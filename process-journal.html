Process Journal

Introduction

This process journal records my progress as a designer throughout the semester; stages of critical thinking and places where I was challenged to think outside the box. Autonomous technology & systems were the topics presented to us to carefully consider. In a world where the integration of technology is currently essential, advancement is inevitable with many autonomous technologies already in use today. Personally, this was the first time I was confronted with the task of having to think about autonomous technologies from a developer’s perspective. My group members were fortunately also in a similar situation, so we were able to begin our research from a similar point of view.

Our group’s aim was to create an application that could help people, making an unfamiliar technology approachable for regular users. We decided to research into medical related fields as nothing in life is important as a person’s health. Our goal ultimately was to tailor medical services accurately and promptly to those who require emergency ambulance services using an autonomous system/technology.

1. Background Research

As a group before we decided on a goal, it took us a long time to decide on a topic. We found ourselves continuously coming back to autonomous vehicles as a solution due to it being the technology we knew most about. Uncertain of deciding on a topic we were unconfident with, our group attempted to create specific solutions first before researching. This led to us stuck in a loop of brainstorming as we had no information or research to check that the answers we thought of were valid solutions.

Figure 1.1 - Sally using an academic database

Seeing our group struggle with arriving to a decision in numerous studio sessions, our tutor advised us to think big instead of trying to come up with an immediate solution as the purpose of the research was to use the information to find a direction. I learnt through this experience that to be a designer, it was essential to be more open minded. Despite having been taught this continuously in previous subjects in this degree, this was the first time I had found myself this frustrated in the ideation process. By experiencing the necessity of this in person it left a memorable impact, something that will now be always remembered in future projects.

Soon after, a broader approach of ‘streamlining emergency ambulance services’ was adopted with each group member being tasked with researching a different aspect that affected ambulance services. Annie researched regular users, Evelyn researched caretakers and I researched professional medical staff. The reason for dividing the research into three different directions was to see if there were any common issues between these three groups. By identifying common issues, we would be able to create an effective solution to cover all groups instead of solely focusing on one. This is especially important in ambulance services that rely on not only the medical professionals but also the users for a smooth operation.

To gain some background information on the field we chose, I researched existing autonomous technology that existed for medicine. The two that I looked at closely were both drone delivery systems, with most autonomous technology in the spotlight being autonomous devices that existed for delivery. Through the usage of autonomous technology, time critical situations can be dealt with as efficiently as possible, as well as saving human resources that are needed elsewhere. This research step showed what was currently possible with autonomous situations and the method of assistance given to people in need, directing me to clearer ideas on how these situations correlated with our goal.

Whilst creating a research plan to better organise myself, I drafted four questions to answer. These questions would help me target what I needed to find out from the medical professionals without asking unnecessary questions as well as make the most of my time limit of two weeks for research.

  • How are ambulances used today?
  • How do people interact with ambulances?
  • How do the needs and usages shift depending on the user’s perspective (patient vs medical professional?)
  • How can the ambulance service be improved?

My core users are medical professionals who work with or directly interact with ambulances.

Interviews

Emergency Department (ED) Nurse

Lesha Sands

Deals with ambulances daily and her main role is to assess patients off ambulances and triage them. Communicates with medics inside the ambulance as the patient is transferred to alert the hospital of their state. Wants a more effective method of communication with EMTs in the ambulance.

Emergency Department (ED) Intern

Josephine Gunawan

She works as a general practitioner in the ED and only transported a patient to another hospital once. This occurred as their hospital did not have the correct facilities. She was concerned during this occurrence as she wasn’t warned previously about accompanying the doctor and did not have all medical information available for this specific case when asked questions from the caretaker.

Registered Nurse

Andrea De Leon

Only interacts with ambulances as a primary caregiver of a patient. Considers ambulances limiting as they are only equipped with essential supplies & equipment to handle any kind of situation. Would ideally like to tailor each ambulance transport to the patient’s personal needs.

Emergency Medical Transporter (EMT)

Evan Zhou

He is concerned about the road and traffic state when driving. To avoid complicated roads, he drives slightly longer distances and his most stressful aspect of the job is driving the ambulance. Urgent and non-urgent cases being segregated is vital and the ignorance of what counts as a ‘true emergency’ wastes resources and the team’s time.

Medical Scribe

Stephanie Chang

Interacts with ambulances as a caller with communication being a big focus in her job. Has faced problems due to language barriers as her work environment has many Asian immigrants, observing numerous scenarios where many patients resort to hand motions to convey their needs. Views communication barriers as an immensely predominant issue in healthcare.

By interviewing the core users (medical professionals) who have the most knowledge over how the system functions, we can understand their needs. Through examining the interviews, it will be easier to identify priorities and problems that they face as well as if they would find the data valuable. On reflection, I was able to have a more personal understanding of their needs and wants as interviews are a more intimate method of gathering data as it involves speaking with a single person.

Organising the data lead to three main issues that were shared by some of the interviewees:

  • Resources (equipment)
  • Manpower (staff)
  • Communication

Surveys

As five people were a rather small sample size, I decided the best next step would be to get more opinions and insights from other people within the industry. Due to time constraints, it was impossible to actively and effectively locate more core users for interviews. To reach a broarder number of people who qualified, an online survey was created with the issues found.

All participants were part of the user group and were found by contacting previous interviewees with a request to pass it to any willing coworkers. Other participants were gathered online through forums. The questions focused on concerns and the user experiences currently felt by participants to help identify further the required needs.

In total there were 47 responses to the survey with two main sections of questions.

When observing answers for key concerns regarding the ambulance service the two main issues are resource and time sensitive. Previous research on existing technology confirmed these responses as being prevalent in daily medical scenarios; not being limited to solely extreme cases.
